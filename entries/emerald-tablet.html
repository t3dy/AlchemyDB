## Emerald Tablet Emerald Tablet

The **Emerald Tablet** (Lat. *Tabula Smaragdina*) is a brief cryptic text attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, regarded by alchemists as the foundational scripture of their art. The earliest known version appears in Arabic sources from the eighth or ninth century, though the text claims far greater antiquity, purporting to be inscribed on an emerald tablet discovered in the tomb of Hermes by Alexander the Great or Apollonius of Tyana. The Latin translation that circulated widely in medieval Europe begins with the famous declaration "Verum sine mendacio, certum et verissimum" ("True, without falsehood, certain and most true"), followed by a series of enigmatic pronouncements about the unity of the cosmos and the process of creation. Its most celebrated maxim—"Quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius" ("That which is below is like that which is above")—became the cornerstone of alchemical cosmology, asserting a fundamental correspondence between celestial and terrestrial realms, macrocosm and microcosm.

The Tablet's thirteen or fourteen brief sentences (depending on the version) describe a process of cosmic generation that alchemists interpreted as instructions for the Great Work. The text speaks of "the father" as the Sun, "the mother" as the Moon, the substance being "carried in the belly of the wind" and "nursed by the earth," and the separation of "the subtle from the gross" with "great sagacity." Medieval commentators like Roger Bacon and Albertus Magnus wrote extensive glosses attempting to decode these phrases as laboratory procedures, while Renaissance Hermeticists like Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico della Mirandola read them as philosophical and theological principles. The text's deliberate obscurity made it infinitely interpretable, allowing each generation of alchemists to discover their own meanings within its gnomic utterances.

Modern scholarship, particularly the work of Julius Ruska and later scholars like Kevin van Bladel, has traced the Tablet's transmission from Arabic sources through Latin translations, revealing how the text evolved and accreted new meanings across linguistic and cultural boundaries. The Tablet's influence extended far beyond alchemy proper: Isaac Newton produced multiple translations and commentaries, seeing in it confirmation of his own theories about universal gravitation and the active principles in nature. The text became a touchstone for Western esotericism more broadly, cited by Rosicrucians, Freemasons, and nineteenth-century occultists as proof of an ancient prisca theologia. Its enduring appeal lies in its compression of vast cosmological claims into a handful of aphoristic sentences, each dense with implication yet resistant to definitive interpretation, making it perhaps the most influential alchemical text never to provide a clear recipe.
